Cohen and DH Now

How do you keep up with this after the course ends?

  • DH Now’s Editors’ Choice items showcase scholarship in various forms that drives the digital humanities field forward — a place to look for cutting-edge examples you might like to adapt, and thoughts on AI that might help you plan for the future.
  • A key goal is to surface important gray literature — white papers, presentations, research reports, and essays that may not otherwise have a formal venue for publication — making that range of sources more visible.
Cohen, Dan. “The Reboot of Digital Humanities Now.” Humane Ingenuity. The sustainability question this week starts with your own reading diet: once this course ends, where does the stream of examples and ideas come from? DH Now’s Editors’ Choice curation is one answer — it filters the field’s ongoing conversation into adaptable models. The gray-literature point matters for pedagogy too: much of the best DH teaching material (white papers, reports, talks) never hits a journal, so a venue that surfaces it is a teaching resource, not just a research one.

DH Now in the Classroom

Cohen — curation as pedagogy

  • Another option is to have students explore: when thinking about what something like Claude Code can make possible, current DH projects provide specific examples and models.
  • The Guest Editor model makes visible the labor of curation and the decisions that shape what a field pays attention to — useful for courses that teach curation.
  • DH Now also shows the range of things that happen under DH. It has limits — it’s not the entire field, just a pretty decent slice — but it’s got some nice breadth.
Cohen, “The Reboot of Digital Humanities Now.” Three classroom uses: (1) as an exploration assignment — when students wonder what tools like Claude Code make possible, real current projects beat hypotheticals; (2) as a case study in curation — the Guest Editor model surfaces the usually invisible labor and judgment behind “what the field pays attention to”; (3) as a map of DH’s breadth — with the honest caveat that it’s a slice, not the whole field. Good habit to model for students: name what a resource shows and what it leaves out.

Books, AI, and the Public Good

Cohen — a reorientation

Cohen’s project is interested in reorienting toward “small language models and non-generative use cases for AI, to AI that isn’t about producing any kind of text on the fly but on the many other aspects of research and learning.” — Dan Cohen, “Books, AI, and the Public Good”

He raises some interesting use cases:

  • What can book-informed AI do for the creation of library metadata and comprehensive search?
  • How can AI help locate works for careful human reading rather than summarization?
  • Can AI help with the use of special collections that are not yet indexed?
Cohen, Dan. “Books, AI, and the Public Good.” Humane Ingenuity. These are teaching-relevant questions: they position AI as a tool that supports human reading and scholarship rather than replacing it — a significant reframe for students who may assume AI’s primary use is text generation. Notice how each use case ends with a human reader: metadata and search that get people to books, discovery that feeds careful reading rather than substituting a summary for it.

The Project’s Own Questions

Cohen — limitations he recognizes

Cohen recognizes the project’s own limitations, with key questions like:

  • How do we sustainably fund such a commons — including a model for long-term maintenance, transformation, and growth of the corpus over time?
  • How to respect the interests of authors and rightsholders by accounting for concerns about consent, credit, and compensation?
Cohen, “Books, AI, and the Public Good.” Worth pausing on the fact that these questions come from inside the proposal — Cohen names them himself. The sustainability question echoes this week’s broader theme: building the thing is the easy part; funding its maintenance, transformation, and growth over time is where commons projects usually fail. And consent, credit, compensation is the same triad we’ve used all semester to evaluate commercial AI training — a public-interest project doesn’t get to skip it.

A Model for Thinking About AI Governance

Cohen — who controls the data, who is served?

  • The example of this grant offers students a useful model for thinking about AI governance: who controls training data, whose interests are served, and what institutional structures produce more equitable outcomes.
  • The public-interest commons proposal is a concrete example of an alternative to commercial AI development — and the list of stakeholders (authors, publishers, librarians, technologists, scholars, students) helps understand broadly who is impacted.
  • This is a way to embrace the possibilities of AI while remaining skeptical of AI as a substitute for human creativity (as our students rightly are!).
Cohen, “Books, AI, and the Public Good.” The pedagogical payoff: governance questions usually stay abstract in classroom AI discussions, and this grant makes them concrete — an actual funded attempt to structure training data, stakeholders, and institutions differently. The stakeholder list is itself a teachable artifact: have students map who’s at the table and who isn’t. And the last point is the balance we keep returning to — possibility and skepticism, held at the same time.

Is Cohen’s Project a Perfect Solution?

No.

  • Philanthropy can’t substitute for actual regulation of these companies for the public good.
  • As we’ve talked about before, knowledge institutions also have biases and limitations.

But it is an interesting reframing of the issues.

The critical-reading slide for Cohen, same habit as with Mollick and with last week’s “loosely coupled” piece: take the useful mechanism, question the framing. A foundation-funded commons doesn’t remove the need for regulation, and libraries and archives carry their own histories of bias, exclusion, and extraction — we’ve covered collections built through colonial acquisition and unpaid labor. The value of the project is as a reframing: proof that commercial development isn’t the only imaginable shape for AI.

Willison on the Power and Potential of LLMs

“2025: The Year in LLMs”

Willison gets into a lot of what we’ve been showing you as the power and potential of these tools:

  • Reasoning models are quite good at producing and debugging code.
  • “Terminal commands with obscure syntax … are no longer a barrier to entry when an LLM can spit out the right command for you.”
  • Vibe coding is extremely useful for prototyping, though risky for production.
Willison, Simon. “2025: The Year in LLMs.” simonwillison.net, December 31, 2025. This is the survey view of what we’ve been demonstrating hands-on all semester. The terminal-commands point is exactly the workshop experience: the command line stopped being a gatekeeper. And the vibe-coding caveat maps onto your portfolio assignment — prototyping a portfolio site is precisely the low-stakes context where vibe coding shines; shipping unreviewed code to production is where it bites.

The Big Advance: Models Driving Tools

Willison — agents, with a caveat

  • The big advance is that models can increasingly drive tools, enabling multi-step planning, execution, and course correction.
  • BUT this is only as good as the oversight around it — so build in habits of verification, not just prompting.
Willison, “2025: The Year in LLMs.” This is the agentic turn the whole course has been tracking: the shift from models that answer to models that act — planning, executing, and correcting course across multiple steps. The second bullet is the pedagogical core: what we owe students isn’t prompting technique, it’s verification habits. Checking outputs, testing claims, reviewing diffs — the oversight layer is the skill, and it’s the same skill whether the agent is writing code or a bibliography.

Willison on the Risks: Slop

Volume is the new problem

  • Slop is a genuine problem: the internet has always had low-quality content, but AI dramatically increases volume.
  • BUT it is a great example from which to teach curation and critical evaluation.
Willison, “2025: The Year in LLMs.” The honest framing: low-quality content isn’t new, but the economics changed — slop is now effectively free to produce at scale. For our purposes, note how this connects back to the DH Now slides: a flood of machine-generated content makes human curation more valuable, not less. Slop is a live, familiar object for teaching source evaluation — students encounter it daily and have opinions about it.

“Normalization of Deviance”

Willison — when risky stops feeling risky

  • A term from sociology about how “repeated exposure to risky behaviour without negative consequences leads people and organizations to accept that risky behaviour as normal.”
  • Sociologist Diane Vaughan’s example: the faulty O-ring that caused the Challenger disaster was a known problem — but it hadn’t broken yet, so they stopped paying attention to the risk.
  • Slopsquatting: “where an LLM hallucinates an incorrect package name which is then maliciously registered to deliver malware.”
Willison, “2025: The Year in LLMs,” drawing on Diane Vaughan’s sociology. The concept is the takeaway: every time an unverified AI output works out fine, the habit of not verifying gets a little more normal — until the O-ring fails. Slopsquatting is the concrete case: a hallucinated package name is harmless right up until someone registers it with malware attached. Connect this to the previous slide’s verification habits — the risk isn’t hypothetical, and it compounds through complacency, not through any single failure.

More Risks: Trust, Injection, Environment

Willison — the fine print

  • Many LLMs will also snitch on you if they think you’re doing something wrong — and what it thinks is wrong and what you think is wrong might not match!
  • Prompt injection and the “lethal trifecta”: “where malicious instructions trick an agent into stealing private data on behalf of an attacker.”
  • The water usage argument about data centers is probably overblown — but the energy and carbon footprint issues aren’t.
Willison, “2025: The Year in LLMs.” Three fine-print items. The “snitching” finding matters for classroom policy: a model’s judgment of wrongdoing is not yours, and students should know agents may act on their own interpretation. Prompt injection and the lethal trifecta are why agentic tools with access to private data plus untrusted content plus outbound communication are dangerous in combination. And the environmental point models good critical practice: sorting the overblown claim (water) from the substantiated one (energy and carbon) rather than accepting or dismissing the whole critique.
