On Holding Back the Strange AI Tide

Mollick — nobody ordered this

“Teachers did not want to see almost every form of homework instantly be solvable by a computer. Employers did want highly-paid tasks that are only meaningful when done by humans (performance reviews, reporting) to be done by machines instead. Government officials did not want a perfect disinformation system released without any useful countermeasures. Released without a manual, no one really even knows what these tools are fully capable of. The world got much stranger, very quickly.” — Ethan Mollick, “On Holding Back the Strange AI Tide”
Mollick, Ethan. “On Holding Back the Strange AI Tide.” One Useful Thing. The opening move: none of the stakeholders got what they wanted. Note the deliberate asymmetry in the middle sentence — employers did want the automation, just not of the tasks whose meaning depends on a human doing them. And “released without a manual” is the condition this whole course has been operating under: we’ve spent a semester writing the manual for our own classrooms because nobody shipped one.

Mollick, More Recently

“The Twilight of the Chatbots” — feeling the curve as shocks

“Being on an exponential means each change over a fixed window is larger than the one before it. If your organization wrote an AI plan any time before the winter of 2025, it described a system that could do a couple of hours of work with a fairly high error rate. A few months later, you can get sixteen hours or more of work from a single prompt. … We are very bad at feeling exponentials from the inside, and we are currently inside one.” — Ethan Mollick, “The Twilight of the Chatbots”
Mollick, Ethan. “The Twilight of the Chatbots.” One Useful Thing. (Supplemental to this week’s readings — his more recent take.) Full middle of the quote, elided on the slide: “This is why AI keeps feeling like it is making leaps, even though it is a curve on a graph, we keep experiencing a steady doubling of capability as a series of shocks.” The practical implication for teaching: any AI policy or course design has a shelf life measured in months, not years. That’s the curricular-sustainability argument from last week restated as a warning — build courses that can absorb the next doubling, because it’s coming either way. Worth naming that this course’s own materials will date the same way.

Trying to Stop AI from Being Weird

Mollick — why the policies won’t work

“So, it is not surprising that so many people are trying to stop AI from being weird. Everywhere I look I see policies put in place to eliminate the disruption and weirdness that AI brings. These policies are not going to work. And, even worse, the substantial benefits of AI are going to be greatly reduced by trying to pretend it is just like previous waves of technology.” — Ethan Mollick, “On Holding Back the Strange AI Tide”
Mollick, “On Holding Back the Strange AI Tide.” The claim to sit with: bans and detection-and-punishment policies treat AI like calculators or Wikipedia — a bounded tool that slots into existing structures. Mollick’s argument is that the weirdness is the technology, and policy built on pretending otherwise fails twice: it doesn’t stop the disruption, and it forfeits the benefits. Connect back to the AI-policy exercise from earlier in the semester — the policies students drafted tried to engage the weirdness rather than eliminate it.

The Future of Education in a World of AI

Mollick’s “fairly safe bets” — written back in April 2023

  • “AI cheating will remain undetectable and widespread.” Research has shown that, even in theory, it is impossible to build a perfect AI detector.
  • “AI technology will remain ubiquitous.” Unprecedented writing and analytical power, available to everyone.
  • “AI tutoring will be excellent, but not a replacement for classrooms.” Classrooms still provide practice, collaboration, socialization, and support from instructors.
Mollick, Ethan. “The Future of Education in a World of AI.” One Useful Thing, April 9, 2023. Note the date: this is one of the oldest pieces we’ve read all semester, written weeks after GPT-4 launched — and all three bets have held. That’s worth connecting to the exponential slide a moment ago: these predictions aged well precisely because they didn’t depend on where the capability curve stopped. His own classroom experiments showed even small amounts of editing defeated AI detection software — the anti-detection argument underneath every AI policy we’ve drafted this semester. And the third bet is the one that matters for your teaching statements: the classroom’s value was never just content delivery.

Enter the Calculator

Mollick — the last time cheating got easy

  • A mid-1970s survey found 72% of teachers and laymen did not approve of 7th-grade students using calculators.
  • A few years later, 84% of teachers wanted calculators in their classrooms — but only 3% worked at schools that provided them.
  • “A practical consensus was achieved. Math education did not fall apart.”
  • “Just as calculators did not replace the need for learning math, AI will not replace the need for learning to write and think critically.”
Mollick, “The Future of Education in a World of AI,” drawing on Sarah Banks’s history of calculators in schools. The parallels are uncanny: worries about dependence, lost basic skills, and the inability to see students’ work (calculators didn’t log the buttons pressed) — all now familiar as AI objections. The arc — panic, enthusiasm without institutional support, then practical consensus — took roughly two decades. One tension to name aloud: in “Strange AI Tide,” Mollick warns against pretending AI is “just like previous waves of technology,” so even his own calculator analogy should be held loosely — useful for the shape of adaptation, not the scale of it.

A More Fundamental Transformation

Mollick — the positive vision

  • Lectures rely on passive learning; the research consensus favors active learning — but quality resources and teacher time keep it rare.
  • AI tutors plus flipped classrooms: personalized content delivery at home, class time freed for meaningful interaction. (“Why raise your hand in class when you can ask ChatGPT a question?” — one of his students)
  • “It may force us to change models, but it will be in a way that ultimately enhances learning and reduces busywork.”
Mollick, “The Future of Education in a World of AI.” The constructive half of the argument: the reason lecturing has survived despite the evidence for active learning is a resource problem — flipped and active models are expensive in teacher time and materials — and AI attacks exactly that constraint. His closing section (“A Time of Uncertainty”) concedes the changes will infiltrate classrooms unpredictably, but argues educators have adapted before and should steer by a positive vision. That’s the through-line to the next slide: adaptation over resistance — and the question of who does the steering.

No policy can stop education from changing…

…but we do need to work together if we want to be the ones who control the tide

The course’s closing argument, in one line. Mollick establishes that holding the tide back fails; the move we’ve been making all semester is the next step — if change is coming regardless, the question is whether educators collectively shape it or have it shaped for them. “Work together” is doing real work here: individual instructors improvising policies classroom-by-classroom don’t set the direction. Institutions, disciplines, and communities of practice — like the NEH cohort this course belongs to — might. That sets up the next slide: because there are other parties very interested in controlling that tide.

Currently, Anthropic Wants to Own the Classroom

Who else is shaping the tide?

Anthropic, “Introducing Claude for Teachers” (anthropic.com/news/claude-for-teachers; image from the announcement). The concrete example of the previous slide’s stakes: while educators debate policy, AI companies are building the classroom products directly — teacher-facing tools, education-specific offerings, institutional partnerships. This isn’t (only) sinister, and we’ve used these companies’ tools all semester — but recall Cohen from the last two weeks: who controls the infrastructure, whose interests are served, and what alternatives (public commons, local models) exist? If educators don’t organize a position, the default is that the companies’ vision of AI-in-education wins by shipping first.
