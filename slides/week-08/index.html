Week 8
Imagining
the Syllabus

ENG 6813 · Salter & Stanfill

The syllabus week: not just a contract or schedule but a pedagogical document that communicates values, sets expectations, and shapes the student experience. As you finalize the Signature Assignment, start imagining the full arc of the course it belongs to. The readings (Cohen; McGinn & Coats; Mollick) all argue for weaving DH and AI into the curriculum rather than bolting them on — which is exactly the design problem the Signature Assignment asks you to solve. No discussion post this week; focus on the assignment.

The Crisis Context

Scott Cohen — the humanities’ branding problem

The humanities are framed as useless — yet they teach exactly the things people think college graduates should know.

“Despite documented demand for critical thinking and creative problem solving, … the stock of the ‘liberal arts’ as a signifier declined and a narrow definition of ‘return on investment’ emerged as the norm.” — Cohen

Cohen suggests DH is a way to “approach the twenty-first-century emergencies roiling our campus.”

Cohen, Scott. "Digital Humanities across the Curriculum, or How to Wear the Digital Halo." What We Teach When We Teach DH. The gap is between what the humanities actually deliver (critical thinking, creative problem solving) and how they’re perceived (a poor ROI). Cohen’s move is to treat that perception crisis as something DH can intervene in — not by abandoning humanities values but by making them visible.

Leveraging the Halo

Cohen — redirecting the energy around “the digital”

“Even if many people did not know precisely what the ‘digital’ did or the future it would create, they were certain it was good and that the institution needed to do more in its name.” — Cohen

“Digital” was a buzzword people threw resources at — the halo. (AI is often functioning that way right now.)

Cohen redirected that energy and urgency “into incorporating elements of digital humanities pedagogy into the curriculum across the college.”

Cohen. The strategic insight: institutional enthusiasm for a buzzword is a resource you can steer. Swap “digital” for “AI” and the dynamic is identical today — the halo is real, and the question is whether humanists capture that energy for humanistic ends or let it be defined entirely by others.

Cohen’s Guiding Principles

Cohen — four commitments

  1. Embrace undergraduates as authors, creators, and curators in the digital realm and in a laboratory setting.
  2. Treat projects and partnerships as iterations and experiments — prioritize prototyping over finished or scalable projects.
  3. Stand up an alternative technical infrastructure: a sandbox outside standard IT, with support gauged to small-scale projects.
  4. Invite unique collaborations beyond academics and across the institution’s structural divisions.
Cohen. Note how much these overlap with what we’ve practiced all term: students as makers (not just consumers), prototyping over polish, a sandbox (Claude Artifacts) outside institutional IT, and cross-disciplinary collaboration. The Signature Assignment is a chance to bake these commitments into a real course.

The Digital and the Humanities

Cohen — DH is at home in the humanities

  • DH practices fit traditional humanities: “learning communities, writing-intensive courses, and collaborative assignments and projects.”
  • The learning outcomes are shared: “critical thinking and expression,” preparing “students as critical participants in civil society and democratic practices.”
  • One of the best things we can do is “providing students with a contemporary toolset and habits of mind vital to critiquing the digital culture around them.”
Cohen. The argument is that DH isn’t a departure from humanities pedagogy — it’s a continuation of it by other means. The payoff line is the last one: we’re not just teaching tools, we’re teaching students to critique the digital (and now AI) culture they live inside.

DH Across the Curriculum

Cohen and McGinn & Coats — integrate, don’t bolt on

Both readings argue for DH across the curriculum: building these approaches and skills in as integrated parts of humanities work, not standalone add-ons.

What makes sense in your curricular context?

  • A one-off exercise or workshop?
  • A unit?
  • The whole course?
Cohen; McGinn & Coats. This is the hinge between the two readings and the assignment. There’s no single right scale — the point is to choose deliberately. Your Signature Assignment should name where it lives in a course, which is the “context for delivery” the assignment guidelines ask for.

What Is Born-Pedagogical DH?

McGinn & Coats — DH that begins in the classroom

  • McGinn and Coats coin born-pedagogical DH (bpDH): digital humanities that arises specifically from the classroom — in a context of curiosity, learning, and experimentation.
  • Tool and method selection follows the humanistic questions.
  • bpDH aligns with Vygotsky’s “proximal development”: learning works best when new skills bridge from what learners already know.
McGinn, Emily and Lauren Coats. "Born-Pedagogical DH: Learning While Teaching." What We Teach When We Teach DH. “Born-pedagogical” plays on “born-digital.” The order matters: the humanistic question comes first, the tool second. The Vygotsky connection is the design principle — scaffold from familiar ground (the zone of proximal development), don’t parachute students into the unfamiliar.

Keeping the Humanities in the Digital

McGinn & Coats — balancing the how-to and the why

Stay “close to the core of humanities methods and questions that the teacher already engages.”

“Balancing the ‘how-to’ with the ‘why,’ to make clear what humanistic perspectives are being enabled through the application of new skills and articulating that balance.” — McGinn & Coats

In the end, “the selection of a DH method or platform depends more on the humanistic questions that the teachers want the class to engage with.”

McGinn & Coats. The recurring danger in DH (and AI) teaching is letting the how-to swallow the why — the class becomes a software tutorial. The corrective is to keep asking what humanistic perspective the new skill enables. That’s also your best defense of the assignment to a skeptical colleague.

Start Small. Play. Stay Small.

McGinn & Coats — three principles

  1. Start Small: you don’t have to do everything at once. Try one thing.
  2. Play: cultivate an environment of trying things out (and probably failing sometimes).
  3. Stay Small: you don’t have to do everything. A small intervention may make more sense than a large-scale project.

Let your learning objectives be your guide.

McGinn & Coats. This is permission-giving advice, and it echoes Cohen’s “prototyping over finished projects.” The fear with DH/AI is that you have to build something huge and technical. You don’t. One well-designed, playful intervention tied to clear objectives is the goal.

Make the Objectives Visible

McGinn & Coats — connecting work to goals builds buy-in

“The more that the teachers and the students can see the clear relationship between these objectives and DH—while having a clear understanding about how they will be evaluated for this kind of work, with the assurance that having difficulties does not necessarily mean a failing grade—the more at ease students and teachers will be.” — McGinn & Coats

Link learning objectives to assignments explicitly: students with a standardized-testing, high-stakes mindset may be skeptical of experimentation.

McGinn & Coats. Two levers for buy-in: (1) show how the DH work serves the learning objectives, and (2) make clear that struggling with an unfamiliar tool won’t tank their grade. This is why the Signature Assignment asks for transparent objectives and an evaluation scheme that rewards process, not just polish.

An Attainable Goal

McGinn & Coats — the through-line

“Instilling collaboration and experimentation into humanities methodology is an attainable and valuable goal.” — McGinn & Coats

Regardless of how you bring technology in, collaboration and experimentation are the durable goals — the tools will keep changing.

McGinn & Coats. The keeper: the specific platform is incidental; the habits of mind (collaboration, experimentation) are what last. Design for those and your assignment survives the next tool cycle.

Prompt Your Way to Teaching Tools

Ethan Mollick — lowering the barrier to entry

“With just a paragraph of instructions, teachers can create tools to help their students learn that would have, in previous years, required buying specialized software built by EdTech companies, even assuming that those companies existed.” — Mollick
  • AI lets you do new things without EdTech, a big project team, or years of work.
  • The barrier to entry is pedagogical and subject-matter knowledge — not budget or technical expertise.
Mollick, Ethan. "Democratizing the Future of Education." One Useful Thing. This is the optimistic counterpart to Cohen’s halo: the capacity that used to require EdTech budgets is now broadly available. The catch — and Mollick is clear about it — is that your expertise becomes the scarce input, not the software.

Better and Worse Ways

Mollick — three things to have AI build

  1. An explanation of a concept, with examples.
  2. An interactive quiz.
  3. A game about a concept.

But we do not want to replace the slow, expert work of curriculum development with prompt iteration.

Mollick. These three are good starting points because they’re bounded and checkable. The warning is the important part: prompt iteration is fast, curriculum design is slow and expert — speed is not a substitute for the judgment that goes into a syllabus.

Drafts, Not Finished Products

Mollick — the risk scales with the stakes

  • Whatever you use it for, there are real risks that require careful checking.
  • Low-stakes quizzes and explanations are far less risky than assessments with real consequences.
  • Treat AI output as a draft, not a finished product.
Mollick. The stakes framing echoes Willison on vibe coding from last week: low-stakes, throwaway uses tolerate less review; high-stakes assessment demands more. The constant is human checking — the teacher stays accountable for what reaches students.

Efficiency vs. Access

Mollick — what the real benefit is

One benefit of AI is efficiency — build your Canvas course or slides faster than you could alone.

But efficiency has historically been used to demand more labor from workers, so it’s double-edged.

The real benefit is expansion of access: enhancing your pedagogical and subject-matter knowledge by communicating it in ways you couldn’t on your own.

Mollick. A careful, two-sided reading. Efficiency is a trap if it just becomes a speedup that lets institutions ask for more. The defensible benefit is access — reaching students in modes (interactive, visual, game-based) that were previously out of reach for a solo instructor.

Your Signature Assignment

From brainstorming to one clear, organized assignment

Take the term’s brainstorming about assignments and AI engagement and refine it into one substantial signature assignment for your course — not a low-stakes task like our discussion posts.

  • Something you could share with a hiring committee, or use to excite students about your course.
  • Build in iteration or active learning.
  • Aim for transferable knowledge that feels relevant to students from different backgrounds.

Due July 5 · 200 points. No discussion post this week.

This is the capstone of the first half. The “hiring committee” framing is deliberate — it should be portfolio-quality. Note how the readings feed in: substantial-but-scoped (McGinn & Coats’ Start Small / Stay Small), iterative (Cohen’s prototyping), and tied to clear objectives (the buy-in slide).

Context and Overview

Your assignment should include — (1 of 2)

  • Context for delivery in the course. What readings or earlier low-stakes tasks does this build on? Clarify what can be reused (like our discussion-post ideas) and what should be revised. You don’t need the exact week, but show where students are pedagogically.
  • Assignment name and overview. Write these student-facing, with detail appropriate to their stage — but design the assignment as a whole to be portfolio-facing too: legible to a hiring committee or colleague as evidence of your pedagogical thinking. Be transparent about learning objectives, and link the assignment to students’ goals and needs.
The first two required elements. “Context for delivery” is the McGinn & Coats / Cohen point about knowing where in the curriculum this lives. The dual audience is the key idea: the name and overview are student-facing — the language students actually read — while the assignment as a designed artifact is portfolio-facing, demonstrating your pedagogical thinking to the hiring committee or colleague named back on the overview slide. Writing well for both audiences at once is the real work.

Examples and Criteria

Your assignment should include — (2 of 2)

  • Examples or descriptions of successful work. An example you’ve made that includes all required elements, or aspirational professional work. (In practice you might ask past students for permission — as in Critical Making in the Age of AI — but here, use external models or build your own.)
  • Rubrics or criteria for grading. Bullet criteria or a formal Canvas rubric. Match the format to your outcomes and class scale: formal rubrics suit larger or intro classes; flexible rubrics suit deeper, specialized work like ours.
Elements three and four. The examples requirement is why the next two slides exist — we’ll look at a real model assignment and real student work. On rubrics: the format is itself a pedagogical choice tied to scale, which connects back to “what makes sense in your curricular context?”

Documenting AI Use

Make the process transparent

Specific guidelines for using AI, and documenting that usage. Be explicit about what you expect:

  • Prompts used throughout the process?
  • Screenshots from using an agentic AI tool to accomplish a goal?
  • A reflection on the work, and any biases or limitations encountered?

And add a brief note on your course context and any changes to your vision, so we understand your goals.

The fifth required element, plus the course-context note. The principle: transparency of AI process is part of the assignment design, not an afterthought. Decide what documentation makes the students’ thinking visible to you — and model the kind of disclosure you’d want from them.
