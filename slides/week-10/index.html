The Value of a DH Degree

Hopwood & Roberts — what the credential is grounded in

“The value of DH degrees is ultimately grounded in interdisciplinary training, exposure to a pedagogy rooted in the classroom and hands-on skill building, and preparation for a variety of professional careers.” — Hopwood & Roberts
  • This is true of the T&T and DHAI programs you’re enrolled in.
  • But it’s also true of the kind of DH courses this class is helping you work toward teaching.
Hopwood, Elizabeth and Kyle Roberts. “What’s the Value of a Graduate Digital Humanities Degree?” What We Teach When We Teach DH. The framing move for the week: the same three things that make a DH degree valuable — interdisciplinary training, classroom-rooted pedagogy with hands-on skill building, and preparation for varied careers — are what your own future DH courses should offer students. You’re on both sides of this claim at once: earning the credential and learning to teach it.

Balancing the Digital and the Humanities

Hopwood & Roberts — inquiry first, tools in service of it

  • DH courses prepare “students to work in and with emerging technologies, while foregrounding DH as interpretive knowledge work.”
  • Alongside the technical, “a robust training in DH must foreground humanities inquiry within coursework and research.”
  • Building stuff is cool and fun — but understanding why to build it and how is where the work happens, especially when the building is (to whatever extent) offloaded.
  • Humanities inquiry first; tools in service of it.
Hopwood & Roberts. The balance question every DH syllabus has to answer. Their formulation — DH as interpretive knowledge work — keeps the technical training subordinate to the inquiry it serves. The third bullet is our course’s running theme: as AI offloads more of the building, the interpretive “why and how” becomes a larger share of what we teach, not a smaller one.

DH in the Humanities Curriculum

Hopwood & Roberts — where does your course fit?

  • Where does your course fit? Will students already have technical expertise coming in?
  • In most cases, no: “most students in their undergraduate careers … might only get to take a course or two with DH content or work on a project with a DH-engaged faculty member.”
Hopwood & Roberts. A design constraint for your syllabus assignment: assume your course may be a student’s only DH encounter. That argues against building on presumed technical background, and for making the course self-contained — teach the inquiry and the tools together rather than assuming a pipeline that mostly doesn’t exist.

DH and Career Readiness

Hopwood & Roberts — digital literacies in the labor market

  • They recommend “discussing how traditional humanities degrees and digital literacies can be spoken of (and traded for compensation) in the labor market.”
  • “A perennial anxiety emerges among students, especially those with humanities backgrounds, that they are not learning enough coding” — AI can be a bridge for making technology projects, as coding becomes less necessary and this anxiety takes longer to disappear.
  • Some students “seek demonstrable technical training and skill sets that they can apply to their own research and careers, such as database design, coding, text mining or machine learning techniques, and geomapping.”
Hopwood & Roberts. Career readiness is a legitimate part of DH pedagogy, not a corruption of it — students need language for trading humanities-plus-digital training for compensation. The coding-anxiety point connects to our AI thread: AI assistance lowers the barrier to making technology projects, which means the anxiety outlives its practical basis. But note the last bullet — some students genuinely want demonstrable technical skills, so “AI can do it” isn’t a complete answer either.

Meaningful Work vs. Exploited Labor

Hopwood & Roberts — the ethics of student projects

  • There’s a tension between giving students real, meaningful work to do and not exploiting their labor.
  • Ensure fair credit; consider labor requirements and conditions.
Hopwood & Roberts. Real projects are motivating precisely because they matter beyond the classroom — and that’s exactly what makes them exploitable. If student work feeds a faculty project, a public archive, or an institutional product, the syllabus should address credit, attribution, and workload honestly. This echoes the labor thread from our AI-policy discussions: the same questions we ask about the labor behind the models apply to the labor in our classrooms.

From the Degree to the Job Letter

Albakry — four pitfalls to avoid

Hopwood & Roberts started us thinking about the job market — now here’s the more brass-tacks version:

  1. Show what guides your teaching practice; don’t namedrop theorists.
  2. Use specific anecdotes and examples; don’t write in buzzwords.
  3. It’s good to show your teaching provides options and range — but “learning styles” are debunked.
  4. Beware of stereotyping low-income, first-generation, and other marginalized students; if you go there, do it with your own specific examples.
Albakry, Mohammed. “Writing a Strong Teaching Philosophy Statement in a Job Search.” Inside Higher Ed, February 15, 2024. Four common ways statements go wrong. (1) Committees want your practice, not a bibliography — theory should be visible in what you do. (2) Buzzwords (“student-centered,” “lifelong learners”) are invisible; anecdotes stick. (3) Offering multiple pathways is good UDL practice — but framing it as “visual vs. auditory learners” signals you haven’t kept up with the research. (4) Generic diversity claims can read as stereotyping; specificity is the antidote here too.

The Teaching Statement

Not always required — always useful

  • Teaching statements are not always a required job material.
  • If it’s not required, you’ll discuss these topics as part of your cover letter.
  • It’s sometimes called a “teaching philosophy,” but don’t let that fool you: it should still be evidence-based, not vibes-based.
Albakry / framing. The genre expectations: even when no separate statement is requested, the same material has to live somewhere — usually compressed into a paragraph or two of the cover letter, so it’s worth drafting the full version regardless. And the “philosophy” label misleads people into abstraction; what committees actually want is evidence — concrete practices, observed outcomes, specific classroom moments.

Structuring the Statement

A workable shape

  • Start with an anecdote as a hook.
  • Provide a framework for your teaching emphasis.
  • Offer specific examples of things you do, and how they demonstrate your approach.
  • If you have them, include citations of sources or methods that inform your teaching.
  • Conclude by indicating how you’ll bring all that to the teaching responsibilities of this particular role and institution.
A workable arc: hook → framework → examples → (citations) → fit. The anecdote does double duty — it’s memorable and it is evidence. The closing move is the one people skip: tailoring to the actual position. A statement that could be sent anywhere reads like it was. The five examples coming up each realize this arc differently — watch for the hook and the fit moves in particular.

AI and Knowledge Institutions

Cohen — loosely coupled collaboration

Like the reading from earlier in the semester, Cohen flags the potentials of AI–knowledge institution collaboration:

  • AI gets better underlying data.
  • Knowledge institutions get better ways to explore and work with their collections.
Cohen, Dan. “AI and Libraries, Archives, and Museums, Loosely Coupled.” Humane Ingenuity. The pivot to the week’s third thread. Cohen’s framing is mutual benefit: models trained or grounded on curated library/archive/museum collections are better than models scraping the open web, and institutions gain new interfaces to their own holdings. The operative phrase is loosely coupled — collaboration without absorption, which the next slide makes concrete.

Loosely Coupled, Concretely: MCP

Cohen — configurable access, institutional control

  • One way to concretely do AI–knowledge institution collaboration is through MCP, which allows any organization to provide access to their collections in specific, configurable ways.
  • MCP allows institutions to maintain control over what is accessible, in what format, and to what degree — such as metadata only, not full text, encouraging users to visit the source itself.
Cohen. MCP (Model Context Protocol) is the technical mechanism for “loosely coupled”: instead of handing over a corpus for training, an institution runs a server that answers AI requests on its own terms. The metadata-only example matters — the institution can act as a discovery layer that routes people back to the source, preserving both control and visits. This is the difference between collaboration and extraction.

What Is MCP?

One protocol between AI and many sources

The one-diagram version: an AI application (the host, with an MCP client) speaks one standard protocol to any number of MCP servers, and each server mediates access to its own data — local sources or remote services. For a library or archive, the server is the institution’s: it decides what questions it will answer and with what. We’ve been on the client side of this all semester — the Zotero and Canvas connections in our Claude Code work are MCP servers. Diagram credit: Edwin Lisowski’s “MCP Explained: The New Standard Connecting AI to Everything” on Medium (linked on slide).

Promise and Problems

Cohen — reading the proposal critically

  • Pro: Local models don’t present the same extraction or data-privacy problems, and they use fewer resources.
  • Con: This approach may not be feasible for small institutions with few resources.
  • Con: The improvement of AI outputs is only as good as the quality and variety of the knowledge institution’s corpus.
  • Con: Knowledge institutions are often extractive too!
The critical-reading slide for Cohen. Local models keep data in-house and shrink the resource footprint — genuine advantages. But the vision assumes institutional capacity that small archives don’t have; it inherits whatever gaps and biases the collection itself carries; and the premise that knowledge institutions are the virtuous party deserves scrutiny — collections built through colonial acquisition or unpaid labor are extractive in their own right. Same habit as with Mollick last week: take the useful mechanism, question the framing.
