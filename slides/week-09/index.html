Hype vs. What’s Useful

Johnson & Salter — separating games-for-learning hype from value

  • There has been a lot of hype around games for learning — it matters to separate the hype from what’s actually useful.
  • Mainstream and commercial games aren’t optimized for learning, and they carry assumptions about the “default” player that can be exclusionary.
  • Some educational games are just memorization — a less effective way to learn most topics.
Johnson, Emily K. and Anastasia Salter. “Designing Playfully for a Distant Future.” Playful Pedagogy in the Pandemic: Pivoting to Game-Based Learning (Routledge, 2022). The opening move is deflationary on purpose: “games” is not automatically good pedagogy. Commercial games assume a default player (skilled, able-bodied, with time and hardware), and drill-and-kill “edutainment” often just gamifies memorization. The rest of the reading is about what a genuinely playful pedagogy looks like instead.

Characteristics of Playful Pedagogy

Johnson & Salter — what makes play pedagogically useful

  • Is inclusive and diverse.
  • Questions dominant culture; provides opportunities to think beyond it.
  • Deemphasizes or reframes failure — success is defined as improving or learning from failure.
  • Promotes questioning, exploration, and creative solutions.
  • Presents unstructured, complicated problems to solve.
  • Follows constructivist (or socio-constructivist, humanist, or connectivist) learning theory.
Johnson & Salter. This is the working definition for the week. Note that “playful” is not the same as “a game” — it’s a stance: low-stakes failure, open-ended problems, and inclusion built in. The learning-theory line matters because it grounds play in constructivism (students building understanding through experience) rather than treating it as decoration.

Create Games for Your Course

Johnson & Salter — why build playful artifacts

“Art, pretend, and play allow us to imagine: to try on new ways of thinking and to fail without detrimental consequences. These are the key aspects of games and playful learning that should be the focus of our pedagogy.” — Johnson & Salter, p. 111
  • Games let students consider perspectives, and low-stakes failure is built in.
  • Games can let you frontload labor: once it’s built, using it in class doesn’t take as much effort.
Johnson & Salter. The keeper phrase is “fail without detrimental consequences.” The second bullet is the practical hook for busy instructors: a well-built playful artifact is reusable — the effort is upfront, not every semester. The next few slides are live examples of playful artifacts to try on.

Have Students Create Games

Johnson & Salter — making as learning

“Creating their own games to teach or demonstrate a course concept provides students with autonomy and responsibility for their own education as well as motivation that is likely to keep them engaged in the learning task.” — Johnson & Salter, p. 113
  • Even if the game isn’t very good, students learn in the process of making it.
  • Students can design a game without building it and still get many of the same benefits.
  • Process-over-product — or setting the bar at “proof of concept” — can decrease anxiety.
Johnson & Salter. The pivot from consuming to making. The load-bearing idea is “process over product”: the learning happens in the design decisions, not in the polish. That also lowers the stakes (McGinn & Coats’ buy-in point from last week) — a proof-of-concept or a paper design is a legitimate deliverable. The next three examples are student-scale creative works.

“But I Don’t Know How to Make Games!”

Johnson & Salter — and where AI lowers the barrier

“Like any educational activity, learning games take time and careful thought to design well.” — Johnson & Salter, p. 114
  • This is a place AI can help: offload the building so you can focus on the conceptual and content work.
  • Hesitant to assign a technology you don’t know well? AI can help level that playing field for you and your students.
Johnson & Salter, with a bridge to this week’s AI thread. The honest caveat first: good learning games take real design thought — AI doesn’t remove that. What it removes is the technical build barrier, so a humanist can prototype the idea without first becoming a developer. This sets up the Mollick material on where AI is genuinely useful.

Are We Teaching for Our Students?

Houshyar — Universal Design for Learning

Houshyar’s opening anecdote raises a key question: Are we teaching for what our students need, or for how we learn/were taught?

UDL is “a set of guiding principles that aims to address the diversity of student needs by ensuring that our course and lesson designs are accessible to all students.” — Houshyar
  • UDL is rooted in disability rights.
  • The goal is to “remove barriers to learning … so that students have the agency, freedom of choice, and sense of empowerment to engage with the material on their own terms.”
Houshyar, Sara. “Universal Design for Learning: Pleasure, Accessibility, and the Radical Possibilities of Good Design.” Visible Pedagogy, CUNY Graduate Center, March 20, 2023. UDL reframes accessibility from “accommodations for a few” to “good design for everyone.” The disability-rights lineage matters: barriers are in the design, not the student. The keyword is agency — giving students real choice in how they engage.

The Three UDL Principles

Houshyar — multiple means of…

  1. Engagement — lets students contend with why they’re learning this and develop affective attachments to the content.
  2. Representation — lets students recognize and organize the content they’re presented with in a systematic way.
  3. Action & Expression — enables students to contend with how they’re learning, giving them metacognitive recognition.
Houshyar. The three UDL networks, in plain terms: the why (engagement), the what (representation), and the how (action & expression). “Multiple means” is the operative phrase — not one mandated path, but options. Your AI-policy exercise this week asks you to check your own course against all three.

UDL + Backwards Design

Houshyar — the questions to ask

Backwards Design asks:

  • What do we want students to get out of this semester-long experience? What goals should they accomplish?
  • What activities, materials, or assignments will get them there?

UDL adds:

  • What barriers might prevent students from accomplishing these goals?
  • How can I lower those barriers — e.g., multiple formats, options for assignments?
  • How can I design the course so it fosters a “pleasurable experience” for students and me — opening possibilities for mutual transformation?
Houshyar. UDL doesn’t replace backwards design — it layers barrier-thinking on top. The last question is the one people skip: designing for pleasure, not just compliance. That’s the through-line to the playful-pedagogy half of this week — good design and enjoyable design are the same project.

Limits of UDL

Houshyar — where it gets hard

  • Some of the more hands-on practices work best face to face and are hard to do online.
  • Projects built on peer feedback and individualized approaches don’t scale well to large classes.
Houshyar. An honest limits slide keeps UDL from becoming a slogan. The tension is real: the most individualized, high-touch UDL practices are exactly the ones that strain online and at scale. Worth naming so your syllabus AI policy accounts for your actual class size and modality, not an idealized one.

Concrete, context-specific how-to

UCF Digital Accessibility Guides. This is the practical companion to UDL’s principles: specific, checkable guidance for the artifacts you actually make — headings, color contrast, alt text, accessible PDFs and slides, captions. UCF policy 2-006 requires WCAG 2.0 AA conformance; these guides are how you meet it. When your AI policy touches accessibility, point students here.

Where AI Is Most Useful

Mollick — expert enough to catch its mistakes

AI is most useful where we are expert enough to catch its mistakes — and can’t use it for the very work that makes us experts.

Mollick, Ethan. “15 Times to Use AI, and 5 Not To.” One Useful Thing. The paradox: AI helps most exactly where you already have enough expertise to catch its errors — but you can’t outsource the effortful practice that built that expertise. The flowchart (redrawn from Aleksandr Tiulkanov’s widely shared version) makes the gate concrete: if accuracy matters, you need both the expertise to verify and the willingness to own the misses. That’s a good test to write into a course AI policy.

Volume, Variance, and Assistance

Mollick — two things AI is genuinely good at

  • Volume and variance: sheer numbers of options — more than a person would generate before giving up.
  • As an assistant: simulate a hostile or friendly audience; take on tasks we could do ourselves but shouldn’t waste time on.
Mollick. The two durable strengths: generating volume and variance (brainstorm past where you’d normally stop) and acting as an assistant for bounded, low-judgment tasks — including simulating an audience to pressure-test your ideas. Problems and Limitations

Reading Mollick critically

  • Mollick is a business professor: efficiency and entrepreneurial framings matter more to him than deep, careful thinking and writing.
  • There is a compounding risk of AI errors across chains of tasks — check as you go, not just at the end.
  • The judgment calls matter: is it really a meaningless drudgery task, or is it the work that builds understanding?
A deliberate counterweight so we don’t take Mollick uncritically. His discipline shapes his values — “don’t waste time on drudgery” assumes you can cleanly separate drudgery from formative practice, and in teaching you often can’t. Errors also compound in multi-step chains, so verification has to happen along the way. These caveats are exactly what your syllabus AI policy should grapple with: copyright, accessibility, equity of access, and the labor behind the models.
